Is Manchester United Still a Top Club?

By Soham Choudhury

Introduction¶

Manchester United Football Club, an organization which is recognized across the globe is also one with the richest of histories in its own country, England, and amongst the highest valued sports franchises in the world, according to Forbes.

Despite the financial success of the club, Manchester United have been going through a title drought ever since the year 2013. At the end of the 2012/13 season, which started in August 2012 and ended in May 2013, the clubs longest serving manager/head coach retired after 28 years of service given towards that team. His most impressive accolades include a record 13 English Premier League (EPL) titles since the leagues inception in 1992. With 20 teams competing in the league every year/season, it is fair to say that Manchester United are spoiled by success having failed to win another EPL since 2013. Clearly, the managers absence is a void that the club have not filled and are suffering from it.

Thus, the question arises: Are Manchester United still a top club? Of course, I have already laid out evidence showing that they are doing well financially, at the time of writing, and they have kept their reputation throughout England and other soccer-loving countries. But, I will be focusing on the on-field performances of the team ever since the year 2013 and attempting to determine from those findings if they are indeed still a top club.

We must first start by gathering all the required data from online sources. I have selected fbref.com as my source since they host the necessary league tables and added information for me to perform my analysis later on. However, since league tables are recorded by many different sites and databases, they should have the same common information across all sources.

I have chosen to use a statistic added to the table by myself called Points per Game (PPG). PPG is simply calculated by dividing the points accrued by a team throughout a whole season by the amount of games they played, which is 38 for all teams in every season.

Thus, a teams rank in the final league table remains the same if it was to be sorted by PPG rather than Points. This is a better way to calculate the performance of a team throughout the season since the measurement for PPG is condensed between values of 0 to 3. If I had left the analysis to be done with Points, the values could have ranged from 0 to 114.

As mentioned before, we have made another set of tables which include xG and xGA in order to perform further analysis on the teams attacking and defending scores.

xG and xGA represent how many goals the team should have scored and should have conceded, respectively, across the season. This is calculated through other factors in the game such as key chances created, player form, distance shots are taken from goal, etc. You can learn more about it here.

Therefore, how well a team has performed according to their expectations can be derived from the following equations in the code.

For example, a value of 1 being resulted for Attack Rating (AtkRt) when Goals For (GF) is divided by xG means that the team lived up to their attacking expectations, greater than 1 if they surpassed it, and less than 1 if they underperformed.

Now is a good time to review the meaning behind each column in the tables...

  1. Rnk is the final position of a team in the league for a given season. It is determined by whoever has the most points with 1st being the winner of the EPL
  2. Squad is just another way of saying team or club. It is the name of the sport's team
  3. MP is matches played which is 38 for all teams
  4. W, D, and L stand for wins, draws, and losses. A team gets 3, 1, and 0 points for a win, draw, or loss, respectively
  5. GF,GA, and GD stand for Goals For, Goals Allowed, and Goal Difference throughout a league season. GD=GF-GA
  6. Pts, and PPG: points are used to determine the final place for each team at the end of the season. PPG=Pts/38
  7. Season is the edition of the league that is reported. The league starts in the fall of the beginning year and ends in late spring of the following year.

After standardization, it will be apparent that the teams who have a higher than average AtkRt will have a positive Standardized Attacking Score; this is a result Manchester United should easily achieve if they were to be considered a top club in the EPL, at least in terms of on-field performances.

std_vals = []

for s in atk_tbl["Season"].unique():
    curr_season = atk_tbl[(atk_tbl == s).any(axis = 1)]
    
    # calculating mean and standard deviation
    mean = curr_season["AtkRt"].mean()
    std = np.std(curr_season["AtkRt"])
    
    # associating a standardized score with each team for every season
    for ind in curr_season.index:
        atk_rating = curr_season["AtkRt"][ind]
        if (std == 0): 
            std_atk_rt = 0
        else:
            std_atk_rt = (atk_rating - mean)/std
        std_vals.append(std_atk_rt)

# adding the scores to the attack table
atk_tbl["std_atk_rt"] = std_vals

Deriving a Conclusion¶

Despite the final season, 20/21 season, showing that Manchester United performed above the standard set by the Big 6 clubs, it is hard to refute the notion that they are no longer the top club in the EPL and are struggling to keep pace with the likes of Manchester City. Long gone are the days where Manchester United could display their sheer dominance over rivals and win the Premier League as often as they did with their successful manager. Their performances in attack are simply not up to par to compete with the best of the Big 6 and their defence was seen to be unreliable or inconsistent.

Nevertheless, we have only shown the downfall of Manchester Uniteds on-field showings; their spending power and their value as a global brand suggest that they are still the biggest club in England.

This study, however, cannot be deemed the final judgement on the fate of Manchester United. Different competitions have not been taken into account; for example, Manchester United won a continental competition known as the Europa League in the 16/17 season. That title, albeit not the EPL, is regarded as a major trophy by soccer fans and clubs in Europe. And yes, we did indeed see a rise in Manchester Uniteds performances in the league last season in 20/21 when they finished runners-up. It is also noteworthy that their team is comprised of young talents, some of whom already have tons of first-team experience, and their board of directors are only going to facilitate bringing in players of that calibre.
